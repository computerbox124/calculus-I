THEOREM - The Chain Rule

If $f(u)$ is differentiable at the point $u$ = $g(x)$ and $g(x)$ is differentiable at $x$ , then the composite function $(f \circ g)'(x) = f'(g(x))*g'(x)$ .

In Leibniz's notation , if $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$ then

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{dy}{du} * \dfrac{du}{dx}$

where dy/du is evaluated at $u = g(x)$.


Example

$y = (3x^2+1)^2$
$y = f(u) = u^2 and u = g(x) = 3x^2+1$

$\dfrac{dy}{du} * \dfrac{du}{dx} = 2u *6x = 2(3x^2 + 1)*6x = 36x^3 + 12x$

Intuitive "Proof" of the Chain Rule

$ \triangle u = g(x + \triangle x) - g(x)$
$ \triangle y = f(u + \triangle u) - g(x)$
If $\triangle u \not= 0$ , we can write the fraction $\triangle y/ \triangle x$ as the product

$\dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle x} = \dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle u} * \dfrac{\triangle u}{\triangle x}$

$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = lim_{\triangle x \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle x} = lim_{\triangle x \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle x} * \dfrac{\triangle u}{\triangle x} = lim_{\triangle x \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle x} *lim_{\triangle x \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle u}{\triangle x} = lim_{\triangle u \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle x} *lim_{\triangle x \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle u}{\triangle x} = lim_{\triangle x \to 0} \dfrac{\triangle y}{\triangle x} *lim_{\triangle x \to 0} = \dfrac{dy}{du} * \dfrac{du}{dx} $
The problem with this argument is that it could be true that $\triangle u = 0$ even when $\triangle x not = 0$.

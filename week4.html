DEFINITIONS

The slope of the curve y = f(x) at the point P($x_0$,f($x_0$)) is the number

$m$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$ (provided the limit exists)

Rates of Change: Derivative at a Point

DEFINITION

The derivative of a function $f$ at a point $x_0$, denoted $f'(x_0)$ , is,

$f'(x_0)$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$
provided this limit exists.


Example 2:

$f(t)$ = $16t^2$ The average speed of the rock over the interval beween t=1 and t=1+h seconds, for h>0:

$\dfrac{f(1+h)-f(1)}{h}$ = $\dfrac{16(1+h)^2-16(1)^2}{h}$ = $\dfrac{16(h^2+2h)}{h}$ = $16(h+2)$

The rock's speed at the instant $t=1$ is then $lim_{h \to 0}$ $16(h+2)$ = $16(0+2)$ = 32

Summary

The following are all interpretations for the limit of the different quotient,

$lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$

  1. The slope of the graph of $y = f(x)$ at $x$ = $x_0$
  2. The slope of the tangent to the curve y = f(x)
  3. The rate of change of $f(x)$ with respect to $x$ at $x$ = $x_0$
  4. The derivative $f'(x_0)$ at a point

Vertical Tangents

We say that a continuous curve y = f(x) has a vertical tangent at the point where $x = x_0$ if $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$ = $\infty\ $ or -$\infty\ $ . For example , $y$ = $x^{1/3}$ has a vertical tangent at x = 0 (see accompanying figure):

$lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{h^{1/3}-0}{h}$
= $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{1}{h^{2/3}}$


However , $y = x^{2/3}$ has no vertical tangent at x = 0 (see next figure):

$lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{g(0+h)-g(0)}{h}$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{h^{2/3}-0}{h}$
= $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{1}{h^{1/3}}$

does not exist , because the limit is $\infty\ $ from the riht and -$\infty\ $ from the left.

3.2 The Derivative as a Function


Derivative at a point, Derivative as a function.

In the last section we defined the derivative of $y = f(x)$ at the point $x = x_0$ to be the limit
$f'(x_0)$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h}$

Derivative as a function derived from $f$ by consediring the limit at each point $x$
in the domain of $f$.


DEFINITION

The derivative of the funtion $f(x)$ with respect to the variable x is the function $f'$ whose value at x is

$f'(x)$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$ ,
proved the limit exists.


Alternative Formula for the Derivative

$f'(x)$ = $lim_{z \to x}$ $\dfrac{f(z)-f(x)}{z-x}$ ,


Calculating Derivatives from the Definition

The process of calculating a derivative is called differentiation.

Example:

$\dfrac{d}{dx}(\dfrac{1}{x})$ = $-\dfrac{1}{x^2}$, $x\not$=0




$\dfrac{d}{dx}f(x)$         $f'(x)$ = $lim_{h \to 0}$ $\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$        $f'(x)$ = $lim_{z \to x}$ $\dfrac{f(z)-f(x)}{z-x}$

Notation

$f'(x) = y' = \dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{df}{dx} = \dfrac{d}{dx}f(x) = D(f)(x) = D_xf(x)$

The "prime" notations $y'$ and $f'$ come from notations that Newton used for derivatives.
The d/dx notations are similar to those used by Leibniz.

To indicate the value of a derivative at a specified number $x = a$ ,we use notation
        $f'(a) = \left. \dfrac{dy}{dx} \right| _{x=a}\ $ = $\left. \dfrac{df}{dx} \right| _{x=a}\ $ = $\left. \dfrac{d}{dx}f(x) \right| _{x=a}\ $

        $f'(4) = \left. \dfrac{d}{dx}\sqrt{x} \right| _{x=4}\ $ = $\left. \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} \right| _{x=4}\ $ = $\dfrac{1}{2\sqrt4} = \dfrac{1}{4}$

Differentiable on an Interval;One-Sided Derivatives

A function $y = f(x)$ is differentiable on an open interval(finite or infinite) if it has a derivative at each point of the interval.
It is differentiable on a closed interval [a,b] if it is differentiable on the interior(a,b) and if the limits

$lim_{h \to 0^+}$ $\dfrac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$    Right-hand deriative at a

$lim_{h \to 0^-}$ $\dfrac{f(b+h)-f(b)}{h}$    Left-hand deriative at b


Derivatives at endpoints are one-sided limits.

When Does a Function Not Have a Derivative at a Point?

A function has a derivative at a point $x_0$ if the slopes of the secant lines through $P(x_0,y_0)$ and a nearby point
$Q$ on the graph approach a finite limit as $Q$ approaches $P$.

Whenever the secants fail to take up a limiting position or become vertial as $Q$ approaches $P$ ,the derivative does not exist.
